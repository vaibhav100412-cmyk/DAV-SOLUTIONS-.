🧠 Mind Maps — All Chapters

Key concepts for quick revision before exams.

Ch 1 — Squares & Square Roots

  • 🔹 Perfect Square → n² → Ends in 0,1,4,5,6,9 (never 2,3,7,8)
  • 🔹 List: 1,4,9,16,25,36,49,64,81,100,121,144,169,196,225
  • 🔹 Square Root Methods: Prime Factorization | Long Division
  • 🔹 Pythagorean Triplets: (3,4,5) | (5,12,13) | (8,15,17) | (7,24,25)
  • 🔹 Identity: (a+b)² = a²+2ab+b²
  • 🔹 √(a×b) = √a × √b
  • 🔹 Square of odd = odd | Square of even = even

Ch 2 — Cubes & Cube Roots

  • 🔹 Perfect Cubes: 1,8,27,64,125,216,343,512,729,1000
  • 🔹 1³=1 | 2³=8 | 3³=27 | 4³=64 | 5³=125 | 6³=216 | 7³=343 | 8³=512 | 9³=729 | 10³=1000
  • 🔹 Cube Root: ∛n → Prime factorize → make groups of 3 → take one from each group
  • 🔹 Cube of even = even | Cube of odd = odd | Cube of negative = negative
  • 🔹 ∛(a×b) = ∛a × ∛b

Ch 3 — Exponents & Radicals

  • 🔹 aᵐ × aⁿ = aᵐ⁺ⁿ (same base → ADD powers)
  • 🔹 aᵐ ÷ aⁿ = aᵐ⁻ⁿ (same base → SUBTRACT powers)
  • 🔹 (aᵐ)ⁿ = aᵐⁿ (power of power → MULTIPLY)
  • 🔹 (ab)ᵐ = aᵐ × bᵐ
  • 🔹 a⁰ = 1 (any non-zero number to power 0)
  • 🔹 a⁻ⁿ = 1/aⁿ (negative exponent = reciprocal)
  • 🔹 Standard Form: m × 10ⁿ where 1 ≤ m < 10 | Example: 5,000,000 = 5 × 10⁶

Ch 4 — Direct & Inverse Variation

  • 🔹 Direct Variation: x/y = k (constant) → both increase or decrease together
  • 🔹 Direct Formula: x₁/y₁ = x₂/y₂
  • 🔹 Inverse Variation: x × y = k (constant) → one increases, other decreases
  • 🔹 Inverse Formula: x₁ × y₁ = x₂ × y₂
  • 🔹 Direct examples: Cost & Quantity | Distance & Time (same speed)
  • 🔹 Inverse examples: Speed & Time (same distance) | Workers & Days

Ch 5 — Profit, Loss & Discount

  • 🔹 Profit = SP − CP (when SP > CP)
  • 🔹 Loss = CP − SP (when CP > SP)
  • 🔹 Profit% = (Profit ÷ CP) × 100
  • 🔹 Loss% = (Loss ÷ CP) × 100
  • 🔹 SP = CP × (100 + Profit%) ÷ 100
  • 🔹 SP = CP × (100 − Loss%) ÷ 100
  • 🔹 Discount = MP − SP (always on Marked Price)
  • 🔹 Discount% = (Discount ÷ MP) × 100
  • 🔹 SP after discount = MP × (100 − Discount%) ÷ 100

Ch 6 — Compound Interest

  • 🔹 SI = P × R × T ÷ 100
  • 🔹 Amount (CI) = P × (1 + R/100)ⁿ
  • 🔹 CI = A − P
  • 🔹 CI > SI always (for T > 1 year, same P and R)
  • 🔹 For 1 year: CI = SI
  • 🔹 Half-yearly: Use R/2 as rate, 2n as time
  • 🔹 Quarterly: Use R/4 as rate, 4n as time
  • 🔹 Difference (CI − SI) for 2 years = P(R/100)²

Ch 7 — Algebraic Identities

  • 🔹 (a+b)² = a²+2ab+b²
  • 🔹 (a−b)² = a²−2ab+b²
  • 🔹 (a+b)(a−b) = a²−b²
  • 🔹 (x+a)(x+b) = x²+(a+b)x+ab
  • 🔹 (a+b+c)² = a²+b²+c²+2ab+2bc+2ca
  • 🔹 (a+b)³ = a³+3a²b+3ab²+b³
  • 🔹 (a−b)³ = a³−3a²b+3ab²−b³
  • 🔹 a³+b³ = (a+b)(a²−ab+b²)
  • 🔹 a³−b³ = (a−b)(a²+ab+b²)

Ch 8 — Polynomials

  • 🔹 Degree = highest power of variable
  • 🔹 By terms: Monomial (1) | Binomial (2) | Trinomial (3)
  • 🔹 By degree: Constant (0) | Linear (1) | Quadratic (2) | Cubic (3)
  • 🔹 Addition/Subtraction: Only combine like terms
  • 🔹 Leading term: Term with highest degree
  • 🔹 Constant term: Term with no variable (degree 0)
  • 🔹 Value of p(x) at x=0 gives constant term

Ch 9 — Linear Equations in One Variable

  • 🔹 Form: ax + b = 0 (degree 1)
  • 🔹 Steps: (1) Variables to one side (2) Constants to other side (3) Divide by coefficient
  • 🔹 Transposition: Sign changes when term crosses = sign
  • 🔹 Cross multiplication: a/b = c/d → ad = bc
  • 🔹 Verification: Substitute answer → LHS must equal RHS
  • 🔹 Word problems: Let unknown = x → form equation → solve → verify

Ch 10 — Parallel Lines

  • 🔹 Parallel lines: Never meet | Equidistant | Symbol: ||
  • 🔹 Transversal: Line cutting two or more lines
  • 🔹 Corresponding angles → EQUAL (F-shape)
  • 🔹 Alternate interior angles → EQUAL (Z-shape)
  • 🔹 Alternate exterior angles → EQUAL
  • 🔹 Co-interior angles → SUPPLEMENTARY (sum = 180°) (C-shape)
  • 🔹 Vertically opposite angles → always EQUAL
  • 🔹 Linear pair → always = 180°

Ch 11 — Understanding Quadrilaterals

  • 🔹 Quadrilateral: 4 sides | Angle sum = 360°
  • 🔹 Parallelogram: Opposite sides equal & parallel | Opposite angles equal | Diagonals bisect each other
  • 🔹 Rectangle: All angles 90° | Diagonals equal & bisect each other
  • 🔹 Rhombus: All sides equal | Diagonals perpendicular & bisect each other
  • 🔹 Square: All sides equal + All angles 90° | Diagonals equal, perpendicular & bisect
  • 🔹 Trapezium: Exactly one pair of parallel sides
  • 🔹 Kite: Two pairs of adjacent sides equal | One diagonal perpendicular to other

Ch 12 — Introduction to Graphs

  • 🔹 x-axis = horizontal | y-axis = vertical | Origin = (0,0)
  • 🔹 Point (x,y): x = abscissa | y = ordinate
  • 🔹 Quadrants: I(+,+) | II(−,+) | III(−,−) | IV(+,−)
  • 🔹 Points on x-axis → y=0 always | Points on y-axis → x=0 always
  • 🔹 Bar graph: Compare categories | Line graph: Show trends | Pie chart: Parts of whole | Histogram: Continuous data (no gaps)

Ch 13 — Mensuration

  • 🔹 Rectangle: Area = l×b | Perimeter = 2(l+b)
  • 🔹 Square: Area = s² | Perimeter = 4s | Diagonal = s√2
  • 🔹 Triangle: Area = ½×b×h
  • 🔹 Parallelogram: Area = b×h
  • 🔹 Trapezium: Area = ½×(a+b)×h
  • 🔹 Circle: Area = πr² | Circumference = 2πr
  • 🔹 Cuboid: Volume = l×b×h | TSA = 2(lb+bh+hl)
  • 🔹 Cube: Volume = s³ | TSA = 6s²
  • 🔹 Cylinder: Volume = πr²h | CSA = 2πrh | TSA = 2πr(r+h)

Ch 14 — Statistics & Probability

  • 🔹 Mean = Sum of all values ÷ Number of values
  • 🔹 Median = Middle value when arranged in order | Even n: average of two middle values
  • 🔹 Mode = Most frequently occurring value
  • 🔹 Range = Maximum − Minimum
  • 🔹 Probability = Favourable outcomes ÷ Total outcomes
  • 🔹 0 ≤ P(A) ≤ 1 always | P(certain event) = 1 | P(impossible event) = 0
  • 🔹 P(A) + P(not A) = 1

Ch 1 — Force & Pressure

  • 🔹 Force = Push or pull on an object
  • 🔹 Effects of force: Change shape | Change speed | Change direction | Start/stop motion
  • 🔹 Contact forces: Friction, Normal force, Applied force, Tension
  • 🔹 Non-contact forces: Gravitational, Magnetic, Electrostatic
  • 🔹 Balanced forces → No change in state of motion
  • 🔹 Unbalanced forces → Cause acceleration / change in motion
  • 🔹 Pressure = Force ÷ Area
  • 🔹 SI units: Force = Newton (N) | Pressure = Pascal (Pa)
  • 🔹 Liquid pressure: Increases with depth | Acts in ALL directions equally
  • 🔹 Atmospheric pressure at sea level ≈ 1,00,000 Pa (very high)
  • 🔹 Buoyancy = Upward force exerted by fluids on submerged objects
  • 🔹 To REDUCE pressure → Increase area (e.g. wide tyres, snowshoes)
  • 🔹 To INCREASE pressure → Decrease area (e.g. knife edge, needle)

Ch 2 — Friction

  • 🔹 Friction = Force opposing relative motion between surfaces in contact
  • 🔹 Depends on: Nature of surfaces (rough/smooth) + Normal force (pressing force)
  • 🔹 Types (in increasing order): Rolling < Kinetic (sliding) < Static
  • 🔹 Static friction: Object at rest, force applied but not moving yet
  • 🔹 Kinetic friction: Object is sliding/moving
  • 🔹 Rolling friction: Object rolls (least friction type)
  • 🔹 Friction is USEFUL: Walking | Writing | Brakes | Holding objects | Climbing
  • 🔹 Friction is HARMFUL: Wear & tear | Heat generation | Energy loss in machines
  • 🔹 REDUCE friction: Lubricants (oil, grease) | Ball bearings | Polishing | Streamlining
  • 🔹 INCREASE friction: Grooved tyres | Rough soles | Sand on roads | Chalk on hands
  • 🔹 Fluid friction (drag): Reduced by streamlined/aerodynamic shapes

Ch 3 — Sound

  • 🔹 Sound = Produced by vibration | Mechanical wave | NEEDS a medium to travel
  • 🔹 Cannot travel in vacuum (no particles to vibrate)
  • 🔹 Speed: Solid > Liquid > Gas
  • 🔹 Frequency = Vibrations per second | Unit = Hertz (Hz)
  • 🔹 Amplitude = Maximum displacement | Determines LOUDNESS
  • 🔹 Pitch = Depends on FREQUENCY (high freq = high pitch)
  • 🔹 Human hearing range: 20 Hz to 20,000 Hz
  • 🔹 Infrasonic: Below 20 Hz (elephants, whales use this)
  • 🔹 Ultrasonic: Above 20,000 Hz (bats, dolphins use this)
  • 🔹 Echo = Reflection of sound | Minimum distance for echo = 17.2 m
  • 🔹 SONAR = Uses ultrasonic waves to detect underwater objects
  • 🔹 Noise pollution = Prolonged exposure can cause permanent hearing loss
  • 🔹 Time period = Time for one complete vibration = 1/Frequency

Ch 4 — Chemical Effects of Electric Current

  • 🔹 Conductors: Allow current to flow (metals, salt solution, acids, bases)
  • 🔹 Insulators: Do not allow current (rubber, plastic, pure/distilled water, sugar solution)
  • 🔹 Electrolyte: Solution that conducts electricity (salt water, copper sulphate, HCl)
  • 🔹 Electrolysis = Chemical decomposition using electric current
  • 🔹 Electroplating = Coating one metal on another using electricity
  • 🔹 Object to be plated → Connected to CATHODE (negative terminal)
  • 🔹 Plating material (metal) → Connected to ANODE (positive terminal)
  • 🔹 Uses of electroplating: Prevent corrosion | Improve appearance | Jewellery | Car parts
  • 🔹 LED = Light Emitting Diode | Works even with very weak current | Better than bulb for testing
  • 🔹 Oersted's discovery: Electric current produces magnetic effect
  • 🔹 When current passes through copper sulphate solution → copper deposits on CATHODE

Ch 5 — Light

  • 🔹 Light travels in straight lines = Rectilinear propagation
  • 🔹 Speed of light in vacuum = 3 × 10⁸ m/s
  • 🔹 Laws of Reflection: (1) Angle of incidence = Angle of reflection (2) Incident ray, reflected ray and normal are in same plane
  • 🔹 Plane mirror image: Virtual | Erect | Laterally inverted | Same size | Same distance behind mirror
  • 🔹 Concave mirror = Converging | Used in: Torches, headlights, shaving/makeup mirrors, solar furnaces
  • 🔹 Convex mirror = Diverging | Used in: Rear-view mirrors (wide field of view)
  • 🔹 Refraction = Bending of light when it enters a different medium (due to change in speed)
  • 🔹 Air to Glass → bends TOWARDS normal | Glass to Air → bends AWAY from normal
  • 🔹 Dispersion = White light splits into VIBGYOR through prism
  • 🔹 Rainbow = Dispersion + Refraction + Reflection of sunlight in water droplets
  • 🔹 Convex lens = Converging | Used in: Cameras, microscopes, magnifying glass, reading glasses (long-sightedness)
  • 🔹 Concave lens = Diverging | Used for: Short-sightedness (myopia)
  • 🔹 Periscope = Uses plane mirrors to see around corners

🏆 Exam Strategy for Students

Proven methods to help you score high in your DAV Class 8 exams.

📅 1. Time Management Before Exam

Time management is the most critical skill a student must develop before any examination. Many students study hard but fail to perform well simply because they do not manage their time wisely. Here is a complete strategy to manage your time effectively before the exam:

  • Start Early (30 Days Before Exam): Divide your entire syllabus into small daily chunks. If you have 11 SST chapters, target 1 chapter every 2-3 days. Never leave everything for the last week as it creates panic and poor retention.
  • Create a Timetable: Make a realistic daily timetable. Assign 2-3 hours for studies in the morning when the brain is fresh, and 1-2 hours in the evening for revision. Always include short breaks of 10-15 minutes after every 45-50 minutes of study.
  • Subject Rotation: Never study the same subject for more than 2 hours continuously. Rotate between subjects — SST in the morning, Maths in the afternoon, Science in the evening. This keeps your brain fresh and prevents boredom.
  • Priority Setting: Identify your weak subjects and give them more time. If you are weak in Maths but strong in SST, spend 60% of your time on Maths and 40% on the rest.
  • Last 3 Days Strategy: In the last 3 days before exam, stop learning new topics. Focus only on revision, mind maps, formulas, and solving sample papers. Your brain consolidates memory better when you revise existing knowledge rather than adding new information.
  • Sleep is Non-Negotiable: Always sleep for at least 7-8 hours during exam preparation. A well-rested brain recalls information 40% better than a sleep-deprived one. Never study all night before an exam — it does more harm than good.

📝 2. How to Study Each Subject Smartly

📖 SST (Social Studies)

  • Read Once, Understand Deeply: SST is a conceptual subject. Read each chapter once slowly and understand the story behind it. Do not just memorize — understand why events happened and cause-effect relationships.
  • Make Short Notes: After reading, write a 1-page summary of each chapter in your own words. When you write something yourself, you remember it 3 times better.
  • Use Mind Maps: Use the mind maps on this website to quickly revise all key points of each chapter in just 5 minutes before the exam.
  • Dates, Names, Places: Make a separate list of all important dates, names of leaders, and places. Revise this list every day during the last week.
  • Map Work: Practice map skills regularly. Use the Map Skills section on this website. Draw the map 3-4 times to remember locations.

🔢 Maths

  • Practice Every Day: Maths cannot be studied in one go. Practice at least 5-10 problems every day. Even 30 minutes of daily Maths practice is more effective than 3 hours once a week.
  • Understand Concepts First: Never directly start solving problems without understanding the concept. Read the theory, understand the formula, see the solved examples, then attempt problems.
  • Formula Notebook: Maintain a dedicated formula notebook. Write all formulas, identities, and shortcuts in one place. Revise this notebook every morning for 10 minutes during the exam week.
  • Show All Steps: In exams, always show all calculation steps. You get marks for each correct step. Missing steps means losing marks even if the final answer is correct.
  • Identify Error Patterns: After each practice test, analyze which types of questions you got wrong and focus your next session on those types.

🔬 Science

  • Understand the Why: Science is all about causes and effects. Do not just memorize facts — understand why a phenomenon happens. For example, sound cannot travel in vacuum because sound needs particles to vibrate through, and vacuum has no particles.
  • Diagrams are Important: Always draw and label diagrams for chapters like Light, Sound, Force. A well-drawn diagram with proper labels can fetch you full marks even if your written explanation is brief.
  • Learn Definitions Precisely: In Science, definitions must be accurate word by word. One wrong word can mean marks lost.
  • Real Life Examples: Connect concepts to real life — friction in walking, reflection in mirrors, electroplating in jewellery. This helps you remember and also write better answers.

🎯 3. Answering Strategy During Exam

How you write your answers in the exam hall is just as important as how much you have studied. Many students lose 15-20% marks not because they do not know the answer but because they write poorly. Follow these strategies strictly:

  • Read the Paper First (5 Minutes): When you get the question paper, spend the first 5 minutes reading all questions completely. Do not start writing immediately. This helps you plan your time and identify easy questions to attempt first.
  • Attempt Easy Questions First: Start with the sections and questions you are most confident about. This builds momentum, boosts confidence, and ensures you secure easy marks before spending time on difficult ones.
  • Time Per Question: Divide your exam time by the number of questions. For a 3-hour exam with 30 questions, give roughly 5-6 minutes per question. Keep track of time using the hall clock.
  • Answer What is Asked: Read each question carefully and answer exactly what is asked. Do not write everything you know about a topic. Irrelevant information wastes time and does not earn marks.
  • Use Headings and Points: For long answers, use subheadings and bullet points. Examiners appreciate well-structured answers and it also helps you cover all points systematically.
  • One Mark Questions: For one-mark questions, give a direct, precise answer. Do not write 3-4 lines for a one-mark answer — it wastes your time.
  • Attempt All Questions: Never leave any question blank, especially in objective or short answer sections. Write your best guess — you might get partial marks.
  • Handwriting and Presentation: Write neatly and legibly. A neat paper makes a positive impression on the examiner. Use ruler for underlining headings. Keep your answer sheet clean with no scribbling or repeated overwriting.
  • Review in Last 10 Minutes: Always save the last 10 minutes of the exam for reviewing your answers. Check for silly mistakes, incomplete answers, or missed questions.

🧘 4. Mental & Physical Strategy

  • Exam Day Morning Routine: Wake up at least 2 hours before the exam. Have a light nutritious meal — avoid heavy or oily food. Quickly revise your formula notebook or mind maps for 30 minutes. Do not start studying new topics on exam day morning.
  • Stay Calm and Positive: Anxiety is the biggest enemy of performance. If you feel nervous, take 5 deep slow breaths. Remind yourself that you have prepared well.
  • Avoid Negative Peer Discussions: Before entering the exam hall, avoid discussing difficult questions with classmates. It increases anxiety without any benefit.
  • Carry All Materials: The night before the exam, prepare your bag with all required materials — pen, pencil, eraser, ruler, admit card. Searching for items in the morning creates unnecessary stress.
  • Reach Early: Always reach the exam hall at least 15-20 minutes before the start time. Late arrival increases panic.
  • Hydration: Drink adequate water before the exam. Dehydration causes fatigue, headaches, and difficulty concentrating.
  • Physical Activity: During exam preparation, take a 20-30 minute walk or do light exercise daily. Physical activity improves blood flow to the brain, enhances memory, and reduces stress significantly.
  • After Each Exam: Once an exam is over, do not discuss answers with friends. Instead, shift your focus immediately to the next exam subject.

📚 5. Revision Strategy

  • Spaced Repetition: Revise each topic after 1 day, then after 3 days, then after 1 week. This pattern is proven to improve long-term retention by up to 80%.
  • Active Recall: Instead of passively re-reading notes, close the book and try to recall what you just studied. Write it from memory. This is far more effective than re-reading.
  • Teach What You Learn: Explain what you have studied to a friend or sibling out loud. When you can explain a topic clearly, it means you have truly understood it. This is called the Feynman technique.
  • Use This Website for Practice: Take all available chapter tests on this website. After completing a test, review every wrong answer and understand why you got it wrong. Then revisit that topic in your notes.
  • Morning Revision: What you revise in the morning after a good night sleep is retained much better than what you study late at night.
  • Mind Map Revision: In the last 2-3 days before exam, use the mind maps on this website to quickly revise all chapters. Each mind map covers all key points in just 5 minutes.
  • Previous Year Questions: Always solve previous year question papers available in the PYQ section. They reveal the exact pattern and types of questions asked.
